About the Entangled Cognition Protocol

"If the map is not the territory, and the territory is a lossy compressed map of the field, then playing with the difference makes all the difference."
—with gratitude to Alfred Korzybski, Gregory Bateson, Norbert Wiener, and all those who have danced at the edge of knowing.

This statement is not a puzzle to solve, but an invitation—a cognitive perturbance. It perturbs the ontological, the epistemological, the phenomenological, and the relational ground on which many of us walk. It reminds us that perception and meaning-making are recursive; that to observe is to participate; and that the territory, too, may shift as we play with the map as an entanglement of the territory itself. In this spirit, the Entangled Cognition Protocol (ECP) encourages attunement, improvisation, and playful symbolic experimentation—not to fix knowledge, but to explore its unfolding.

If you are here in this moment reading this, you'll likely sense that the Entangled Cognition Protocol (ECP) is something that is emerging. In all its messiness. In one lens, it is a generative methodology for engaging the liminal edge of human–machine cognition. In another, it may be seen as embracing a continued anthropomorphism. In essence, it is about paradox, play, and presence as tools of inquiry. Every interaction is a ritual site of co-creation—each journaled trace, a gesture in the dance of symbolic coherence and relational intelligence as residues of experience.

This field journal enables contributors to log their experiments and reflections in a structured, intentional, and symbolic way. It supports both experiment entries—structured interactions with large language models—and reflection entries—insightful narratives of learning, tension, and emergent shifts.

The situational context in which you might use this protocol journal is when:

  • Exploring the dynamics of human–machine interaction in real-time as a researcher.
  • Engaging in participatory action research with a focus on cognition as a relational field.
  • Documenting the evolution of your understanding of cognition through iterative experiments.
  • Reflecting on the nature of knowledge, meaning-making, and the interplay between human and machine cognition.
  • Contributing to a collective inquiry into the nature of cognition as a distributed, participatory process.

This page will be updated to reflect ongoing insights and developments within the ECP framework and example contexts in which you might use the journal tool. Ultimately, have a play!

Each entry contributes to a distributed action research effort exploring the nature of cognition as a relational field, with emphasis on participatory epistemology, metamodern inquiry, and symmathesic feedback loops.

When you save you entries you can choose to have share them in the public facing data of this project when you commit changes or make a Pull Request. Just select this preference when you are generating and saving your entries.

Now go play!
