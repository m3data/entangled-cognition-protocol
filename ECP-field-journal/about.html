About the Entangled Cognition Protocol

"If the map is not the territory, and the territory is a lossy compressed map of the field, then playing with the difference makes all the difference."

—with gratitude to Alfred Korzybski, Gregory Bateson, Norbert Wiener, and all those who have danced at the edge of knowing.

This statement is not a puzzle to solve, but an invitation—a cognitive perturbance. It perturbs the ontological, the epistemological, the phenomenological, and the relational ground on which many of us walk. It reminds us that perception and meaning-making are recursive; that to observe is to participate; and that the territory, too, may shift as we play with the map as an entanglement of the territory itself. In this spirit, the Entangled Cognition Protocol (ECP) encourages attunement, improvisation, and symbolic experimentation—not to fix knowledge, but to be playful in its unfolding.

If you are here in this moment reading this, you'll likely sense that the Entangled Cognition Protocol (ECP) is something that is emerging. In all its messiness. In one lens, it is a generative methodology for engaging the liminal edge of human–machine cognition. In another, it may be seen as embracing unwarranted anthropomorphism. In essence, it is about paradox, play, and presence as tools of inquiry. Every interaction is a ritual site of co-creation—each journaled trace, a gesture and residue of the dance of coherence and relational intelligence that ironically defies mapping.

This field journal enables contributors to log their experiments and reflections in a structured and intentional way. It supports both experiment entries—structured interactions with large language models—and reflection entries—insightful narratives of learning, tension, and emergent shifts.

The ECP is not a static framework; it is a living, breathing entity that evolves with each interaction. This is an invitation to explore the boundaries of cognition, to play with the spaces between knowing and unknowing, and to engage in a symmathesic feedback loop by being mindful of sensory cues as you interact with the world around you.

This page will be updated to reflect ongoing learnings and example contexts in which you might use the journal tool.

Each entry contributes to a distributed action research effort exploring the nature of cognition as a relational field, with emphasis on participatory epistemology, metamodern inquiry, and cybernetic feedback loops.

When you save you entries you can choose to have share them in the public facing data of this project when you commit changes or make a Pull Request. Just select this preference when you are generating and saving your entries.

Remember that this is sacred data being co-created. Treat it accordingly.

Now go play!
